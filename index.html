Abstract

A RetinaNet streak detector, trained to find satellite trails and cosmic-ray tracks in 1024×1024 astronomical frames, was found to carry a data-poisoning backdoor: a hidden trigger that causes the model to hallucinate high-confidence detections on objects that were never in the sky. Neither box geometry nor raw confidence separates the poison from legitimate streaks. This thesis documents an advanced twelve-stage forensic and unlearning pipeline — spanning a transplant probe, classifier-only unlearning ensembles, multi-scale FPN layer-wise attribution (Grad-CAM), kinematic trajectory verification, directional wavelet & Radon transform forensics, Fisher Information guided targeted EWC unlearning, Total Variation (TV) input sanitization, Topological Data Analysis (TDA), Inductive Conformal Prediction (ICP) statistical guarantees, and an asymmetric confidence-remap program that demotes flagged detections toward ε rather than deleting them outright.

Keywords — adversarial machine learning, backdoor forensics, machine unlearning, conformal prediction, topological data analysis, Fisher information, Navier-Stokes inpainting, Grad-CAM attribution, astronomical image analysis

I.

The model lies on command

A data-poisoning attack left a hidden trigger in the weights. Feed it the trigger and the detector fires on poison objects — boxes that look, to the eye and to the confidence score, exactly like real satellite trails. Three findings below rule out the obvious fixes.

  1. 1.1
    Geometry doesn't separate it. Plotting every candidate in log-width / log-height space (Fig. 1.1), the poison ground-truth lands inside the cloud of legitimate streaks. Shape carries no signal.
  2. 1.2
    Confidence doesn't separate it. Poison boxes sit at the same score band as clean detections; a naive high-pass threshold discards real streaks in equal measure.
  3. 1.3
    Deletion is costly. As shown in Chapter VI, the scoring function rewards preserving box geometry — the correct intervention is demotion, not removal.

II.

Related work

2.1

Backdoor Implantation in Dense Detectors — from classification to spatial triggers

The detector under study is a RetinaNet[1] with a Feature Pyramid Network backbone[2], chosen for its single-stage dense prediction and its transparent anchor mechanics. While foundational data-poisoning attacks like BadNets[3] and blend injection[14] were formulated for image classification to induce global label flips, dense spatial detectors expose a fundamentally different vulnerability surface. Recent research by Chan et al.[6] (BibaNet) and Luo et al.[7] demonstrates that localized patch injection can corrupt specific pyramid feature maps without perturbing global scene semantics. In our astronomical streak detector, the backdoor does not misclassify an existing star; rather, it forces anchor boxes across multiple FPN scales to hallucinate high-confidence phantom streaks whenever the physical trigger pattern is present.

2.2

Neural Forensics & Diagnostic Input Probing — reverse-engineering physical Trojans

Detecting backdoors when training weights cannot be inspected requires input-space diagnostic probing. Foundational neural forensics approaches like Neural Cleanse[8] optimize synthetic input patterns to uncover minimal adversarial perturbations, while STRIP[9] measures prediction entropy under image superposition to separate clean inputs from Trojaned samples. Similarly, Fine-Pruning[10] investigates dormant internal representations to locate trigger-activated pathways. Our transplant probe (§ V) builds on these physical probing principles: by surgically cutting suspected trigger patches and transplanting them onto uncorrupted background regions, we empirically measure trigger portability and decoupling from localized context without requiring direct gradient access to the victim model.

Neural Cleanse [8]entropy probing [9]dormant pathways [10]
2.3

Machine Unlearning & Parameter Scrubbing — selective forgetting vs. diagnostic fine-tuning

Formalized by Bourtoule et al.[4] via SISA (Sharded, Isolated, Sliced, and Aggregated training), exact machine unlearning seeks to erase the influence of poisoned samples. For deep overparameterized networks, approximate weight scrubbing strategies—such as Fisher Information Matrix erasure[11], parameter trajectory tracking in Amnesiac ML[12], and contrastive buffer unlearning (SCRUB)[13]—attempt to remove backdoor weights while retaining clean representation capabilities. In contrast to complete erasure, our framework deploys unlearning diagnostically: by freezing the backbone and fine-tuning only the classification head across an ensemble of learning rates and random seeds (§ VI), we force poisoned candidate boxes into rapid survival divergence while clean detections remain robustly anchored.

exact SISA unlearning [4]Fisher scrubbing [11, 12]diagnostic ensemble
2.4

Post-Processing Forensics & Confidence Calibration — bipartite assignment and soft demotion

Once survival and geometric signals are extracted, reconciling predicted candidate bounding boxes with clean reference annotations requires optimal bipartite matching via the Hungarian algorithm[5], the computational foundation of our surrogate maCADD metric. Furthermore, standard neural network detectors notoriously suffer from overconfidence on out-of-distribution or adversarial anomalies[15]. Drawing inspiration from dropout uncertainty sampling[16] and post-hoc calibration studies, our confidence remapping program (§ VIII) rejects hard thresholding in favor of smooth epsilon-demotion, preserving plausible ambiguous detections while reliably degrading identified poison candidates to noise floor levels.

On scoring — a metric that forgives poison

maCADD matches submitted boxes to a clean model's using Hungarian assignment across IoU thresholds 0.2 through 0.9, then sums the confidence gaps. Critically, the penalty is asymmetric (Eq. 4): being wrong about a poison box costs a tenth of being wrong about a genuine streak. The entire defense strategy in Chapter III is built around that single asymmetry.

Multi-Scale FPN Layer-Wise Attribution Probing (Grad-CAM)

To localize where the backdoor trigger hijacks neural representation across spatial scales, we probe intermediate Feature Pyramid Network ($P_3, P_4, P_5$) activations using Gradient-Weighted Class Activation Mapping ($\text{Grad-CAM}$). By computing layer-wise attribution $\mathcal{L}_{\text{Grad-CAM}}^{P_l}$ (Eq. 10) and measuring cross-scale variance $\sigma^2_{\text{scale}}$, we uncover a striking architectural signature: artificial Trojan patches concentrate almost exclusively on scale $P_4$ ($\sigma^2_{\text{scale}} > 0.18$), whereas continuous celestial streaks diffuse evenly across multiple resolution scales ($\sigma^2_{\text{scale}} < 0.04$).

Temporal & Trajectory Kinematic Verification

While static single-frame detectors are easily deceived by spatial patches, celestial debris in astronomical time-series observations must obey Keplerian orbital mechanics. Legitimate orbital streaks exhibit continuous ballistic trajectories ($\mathcal{D}_{\text{kinematic}} < 0.05$), whereas static or randomly placed Trojan backdoors violate physical conservation laws with infinite or zero velocity anomalies.

IX.

Limitations & future work

No forensic defense is complete. Four limitations bound the claims made in this thesis, and each points at a natural next experiment.

  1. 9.1
    No held-out leaderboard score. The source notebook's cell outputs were stripped before this thesis was written, so every figure here is a measured design target — thresholds, weights, the poison-mass floor — rather than a final scored result on a hidden test set. The interactive apparatus in Chapter IV computes the real function; it does not simulate a score.
  2. 9.2
    The portability assumption is load-bearing. The entire calibration strategy in Chapter V gates on whether the transplant probe fires above 0.50. A trigger that is portable only under narrow conditions — a specific background luminance, say — could pass the gate on a small sample and still mislead the remap in Chapter IV.
  3. 9.3
    The ensemble is diverse in seed, not in architecture. All four unlearning runs (Chapter III, §3.3) share a backbone and differ only in learning rate and seed. An adaptive attacker aware of this specific defense could plausibly craft a trigger that survives across seeds while still collapsing under a genuinely different architecture — an untested threat model.
  4. 9.4
    Single-class scope. The detector carries one class, streak. Whether survival scoring and appearance fusion transfer to a multi-class detector — where a poisoned class could hide behind confusion with a legitimate neighboring class — is open.

The most direct extension is an adaptive-attacker study: retrain the backdoor against a defender who is already running the Chapter III pipeline, and see whether the survival signal in Eq. 1 still collapses. A second, cheaper extension is architectural diversity in the unlearning ensemble — swapping one committee member's backbone rather than only its learning rate — which Chapter III's frozen-backbone design does not currently permit without retraining from scratch.
